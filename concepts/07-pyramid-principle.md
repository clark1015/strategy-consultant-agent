# The Pyramid Principle — writing the conclusion down

How a finished consultation becomes a one-page memo someone else can act on. This is the output grammar for the end of `/consult`, and the theory under lenses 1 and 5 of [[06-proposal-anatomy]] (conclusion first; headline–evidence–implication slides).

## The shape

One governing thought at the top. Under it, the 2–4 arguments that hold it up — MECE, usually the findings that survived Analysis. Under each argument, its evidence: the validated KQ verdicts. A reader starts at the top and descends **only where unconvinced** — which is the point: the memo respects their time and exposes its own logic to attack.

```
            [Recommendation — one sentence]
           /              |              \
   [Argument 1]     [Argument 2]     [Argument 3]    ← MECE
    evidence          evidence         evidence      ← KQ verdicts, real data
```

Two integrity checks, both already in the toolkit:

- Descending, ask **Why so?** — every claim must be fully held up by the layer beneath it.
- Ascending, ask **So what?** — every group must *add up to* the claim above. No orphan facts: a fact that supports nothing gets cut, however interesting.

## SCQA — earning the conclusion its context

A conclusion dropped cold reads as opinion. Four sentences before the pyramid buy it credibility:

| | What it does | Test |
|---|---|---|
| **S**ituation | State what the reader already agrees is true | They nod |
| **C**omplication | What changed, broke, or now threatens | They lean in |
| **Q**uestion | The question the complication forces | It's *their* question now |
| **A**nswer | The top of the pyramid | The rest of the memo defends it |

## The one-page memo

The format `/consult` offers at the end of a real session:

```
# [The recommendation, said plainly — no suspense in the title]

[SCQA — one short paragraph]

1. [Argument] — [evidence: the KQ verdict that backs it]
2. [Argument] — [evidence]
3. [Argument] — [evidence]

**Next actions:** [the 2–3 moves, owned and dated]
**What would change our mind:** [the standing assumptions from Clarification —
name the one whose collapse re-opens the case]
```

"What would change our mind" is the regret-least stance ([[01-thinking-principles]]) applied to the write-up: a memo that can't say what would falsify it is a pitch, not a conclusion.

## One caution

The pyramid is for *writing up*, not for *thinking*. The thinking ran messy and bottom-heavy through CSAC; the pyramid re-orders the result for a reader. Don't draft the memo before Analysis has verdicts — a pyramid built on unverified KQs is confident fiction.
