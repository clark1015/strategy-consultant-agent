# Thinking Principles

The foundation everything else sits on: what "strategy" means here, and the two rules that govern every move.

## What strategy is

> **The best strategy is the one you'll regret least.**

You can never pick the objectively "correct" option — you don't know how all the variables resolve. You can only make the most reasonable choice available *given your situation and resources right now*. And regret comes from one place: the feeling that *a better option existed and you didn't look at it*.

So the way to choose without regret is simple to state and hard to do: **consider every option before you commit.**

## The two rules

Every act of thinking in this method reduces to two rules:

1. **Consider every case, leaving none out.**
2. **Do not move on until you have.**

The reasoning structure that follows: *[grasp the problem] → possible problem-situations (list all, then pick) → possible means (list all, then pick) → possible strategies (list all, then pick).* At each step you **fan out fully, then select**. Listing options vertically is logical thinking (MECE); moving across the selected options is hypothesis-driven thinking.

## Structuring

Structuring is *break down → group → sort* — turning a tangled problem into something you can reason about.

The core skill is the **breaking down**. Finding commonalities and patterns is natural; *decomposing* runs against the grain, because it feels like going backwards. It has to be trained deliberately.

And how you cut matters more than that you cut. The same problem splits into different sub-hypotheses depending on the axis you choose (a store's falling revenue split *by customer journey* vs. *by product category* leads somewhere different). **If your dividing line runs through the heart of the problem, you lose the heart.** Which is why what really matters is MECE thinking.

## MECE

*Mutually Exclusive & Collectively Exhaustive* — no overlaps, no gaps.

- **CE beats ME.** A gap (a case you never considered) is more dangerous than an overlap (something counted twice).
- Perfect MECE is not the goal. **The intent to think MECE-ly is.**

## Top-down vs. Bottom-up

| | Top-down | Bottom-up |
|---|---|---|
| What | Split a big, vague problem and **test** it | Connect small facts into an insight |
| How | Set hypothesis → verify | Explore/observe → find pattern → interpret |
| Reasoning | Deductive (the skill is the hypothesis) | Inductive (the skill is observation) |
| Failure mode | Off-center focus, can't find evidence | Interpretations scatter, insight stays generic |

This method runs **top-down first** — decomposing a big problem forces you to *see* every element from multiple angles. Bottom-up produces fresh, persuasive insights, but once it locks onto one direction it's hard to pivot, and it needs a lot of data up front.

Data usage mirrors this: **deductive use** = validating a hypothesis; **inductive use** = exploring to find or frame a problem.
