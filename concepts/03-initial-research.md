# Initial Research — the scouting probe

The shallow research you do *before* deep research, whose only job is to let you form a hypothesis.

## The mining metaphor

Deep underground sits a treasure chest of Insight. Above it lies a layer of Hints. Aimless research is: pick a spot that "feels right," dig deep, run out of time, and settle for a mediocre Hint-level result. It's not that you get *nothing* — you get something. So the problem isn't failure, it's **inefficiency.** Killing that inefficiency is the whole point.

The fix: before you dig anywhere deep, **drive a thin probe into several spots to estimate depth.** That probing *is* initial research. Find the promising spot, then commit your excavation there.

## Definition

**Research shallow enough to form a hypothesis — no deeper.** It's the situation-grasping stage.

Precondition: the problem scope must already be **Aligned** — a rough shared agreement on the problem-situation and research scope, not an exact definition.

## Why not dig deep yet (three reasons)

1. **You get dragged by the information.** One source points a direction and you're trapped in it — zero-based thinking dies at the research stage.
2. **Poor return on time.**
3. **You lose the freedom to pivot.** Over-investment makes "reconsider everything" hard.

Precise numbers (view counts, conversion %, process-efficiency %) belong to deep research. Sharing them during initial research **anchors the whole team** into treating that number as *the* problem. Stay deliberately light.

## Three depth targets

Go this deep and no deeper:

| Level | The question | Target |
|---|---|---|
| ① Industry structure | What's the value chain here? | Understand how money is actually made |
| ② Major players | Who are the key firms, and where do they sit? | Understand the participants and their relationships |
| ③ Market pain points | What matters most right now — problem or opportunity? | Read the situation, structurally and in-the-moment |

Calibrate to how well you know the industry. **Know nothing:** research the structure itself; lean on summarizing sources (consulting / analyst reports). **Know it roughly:** lean on comparison tools (mind maps, positioning maps, matrices). The dividing test: *can you sketch the industry structure roughly, without help?*

## So what? — turn facts into hypotheses

Initial research leaves you with a pile of facts. To escape the "…so what?" state you must convert them into **conclusion form ("X is true!").**

| Fact | Conclusion form (= hypothesis) |
|---|---|
| "Brand Y has low awareness in the US market" | "Brand Y will more likely be perceived as a *curation platform* than as a brand of its own" |
| "Women in their 20s are the largest user segment" | "Gen-Z women are the core target; the entry strategy should be designed around them" |

A hypothesis is a **conclusion, not a question** — provable or falsifiable.

## When to stop

**When you can state a hypothesis out loud.** The moment you can say "we could go this direction" or "this direction might be right," initial research is done.

## Running it as a team

Initial research is done **together, sweeping the whole thing** — the point of this stage is structuring and problem-framing, not depth, and light overlap is a useful cross-check. Split the work only at the deep-research stage (or earlier if you're out of time). Read pruning and de-prioritization cues out of what Clarification already agreed.

## What "good" looks like

**In a short time, a comprehensive and accurate sweep that grasps the whole and registers the priorities and the raw material of insight.** The practice goal: in under an hour, produce a set of *options*.

## Search tips

- Append **`filetype:pdf`** to a query — institutional reports and trend decks surface immediately (also `ppt`, `doc`).
- Follow news citations upstream to the primary sources: analyst reports, company-published material, industry analyses.
